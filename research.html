Research

Bilingual acquisition · heritage languages · morphosyntax

I study how age and language input interact to shape bilingual grammars — from the first years of acquisition through adult heritage and "returnee" speakers — using Turkish in contact with English and German as a testing ground for theories of morphosyntax.

Grounded in linguistic theory and experimental methods, my work asks what stays stable and what remains flexible in a grammar across the lifespan: why some heritage speakers restructure their first language after intensive childhood exposure to a majority language, and why others who return to a heritage‑dominant environment as adults can reactivate morphosyntax that seemed lost. I work primarily on inflectional morphology, differential object marking, relative clauses, and evidentiality, with a focus on languages that remain understudied in the acquisition literature.

My current project at the University of Konstanz investigates the heritage acquisition, maintenance, and reactivation of Turkish in contact with German and English, extending earlier dissertation work (advised by Silvina Montrul at the University of Illinois Urbana‑Champaign) on Turkish‑American returnee bilinguals.
